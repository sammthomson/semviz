Part-of-speech tags

A part-of-speech (POS) category such “noun,” “verb,” or “preposition” designates a group of words with certain morphosyntactic properties. Several POS categorization schemes, or tagsets, exist for English and other languages.

Dependency parses

A dependency parse represents the syntactic structure of a sentence in terms of binary relations between tokens. For example, a verb is linked to its dependents (arguments/modifiers). Collectively, these relations form a tree or tree-like graph.

Stanford dependencies are a representation that categorizes/labels the head-dependent relation types; these include nsubj (nominal subject), dobj (direct object), iobj (indirect object), aux (auxiliary verb), prep (prepositional phrase), pobj (object of preposition), det (determiner), nn (noun compound modifier), amod (adjectival modifier), advmod (adverbial modifier), and punct (punctuation). Frame-semantic parses

FrameNet is a semantic resource for English that consists of relational concepts/scenarios known as frames. There are two parts to FrameNet:

  • An inventory of over 1000 frames, each mapped to linguistic expressions (lexical units) and structured in terms of participants/props (frame elements). For example, lie, deceive, deception, and hoodwink are all known to evoke the Prevarication frame, whose frame elements include the Speaker (prevaricator), Addressee, and Topic.
  • Corpus sentences that have been analyzed in terms of these frames, either in full or on a targeted basis.

SEMAFOR analyzes the frames and frame elements expressed by a sentence. For example, the sentence

Have you ever lied about the President’s health?

would ideally be analyzed as evoking the following three frames:

  • Prevarication: lied
  • Speaker: you
  • Topic: about the President ’s health
  • Addressee: ?
  • Time: ever
  • People_by_vocation: President
  • Person: President
  • Medical_conditions: health
  • Patient: the President ’s
  • Ailment: ?

In fact, SEMAFOR currently produces the following parse:

HaveyoueverliedaboutthePresident'shealth?

The row below the sentence gives frame names, and the subsequent rows indicate arguments (frame element fillers) for each frame. Argument spans are indicated with red bars.

Note that this parse does not quite match the desired analysis: the wrong frame is chosen for President, and the Topic and Time arguments of the Prevarication frame are missing entirely. Frame-semantic parsing is such a difficult task that automatic parses are rarely perfect.
